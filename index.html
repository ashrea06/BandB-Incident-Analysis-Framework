Backdoors & Breaches — Security Incident Analysis Framework

A structured methodology mapping gamified tabletop simulation directly to the Security Incident Analysis Worksheet.

Goal Statement

The objective of this framework is to bridge the gap between gamified tabletop simulation and structured academic and professional incident analysis. By mapping each stage of a Backdoors & Breaches tabletop exercise directly to the corresponding sections of the Security Incident Analysis Worksheet, we create a learning pathway that transforms gameplay into a rigorous, reusable analytical output.

The tabletop exercise is not treated as a standalone activity. Every element of the game — the incident cards, procedure cards, successes, failures, undiscovered attack stages, and time spent across IR phases — is deliberately mapped to a worksheet section, ensuring that analysts and students leave the exercise with a partially or fully completed incident analysis rather than just an experiential memory of the session.

Understanding the Game — The Core Formula

Each incident card represents a stage in the attack chain, not just an isolated event — it represents a progression of adversary objectives.

Threat Source + Threat Event → Adversary Action (TTP) → Observable Impact = Tactic Achieved

Each incident card encodes all four elements of this formula simultaneously — who the threat source is, what threat event occurred, which TTP was employed, and what observable impact resulted. A successful procedure card does more than confirm detection — it validates that a specific adversary objective was being executed at that stage, telling the analyst how far the attacker had progressed through the attack chain and what was already compromised at that point.

How the Game Structures the Attack Chain

Rather than mapping to all 14 MITRE ATT&CK tactic stages, Backdoors & Breaches intentionally condenses the attack lifecycle into four high-level stages:

  • Entry — Initial Compromise
  • Movement — Pivot & Escalate
  • Data Theft — C2 & Exfiltration
  • Staying Hidden — Persistence

This simplification is deliberate. It makes gameplay manageable, keeps decision-making clear, teaches detection thinking without overwhelming players, and still maps closely to the full MITRE ATT&CK framework. Every incident card revealed during play can be immediately placed within one of these four stages, and that placement tells the analyst where in the adversary's progression the team currently is, which directly informs what containment or eradication actions are most urgent next.

Documenting the Reveal

When you document a revealed incident card in the worksheet, you are recording all four confirmed elements at once: who was responsible (Threat Source), what happened at that stage (Threat Event), how the adversary executed it (TTP), and what evidence your detection capability surfaced (Observable Impact). After the reveal, uncertainty collapses across all four dimensions simultaneously — the analyst has a confirmed origin, a confirmed action, a confirmed technique, and confirmed evidence, establishing exactly how far the attacker had progressed. This is precisely what Section 8 (Vulnerabilities and Techniques) and Section 12 (Lessons Learned) require — a fully attributed, stage-confirmed entry in the attack chain that connects directly to MITRE ATT&CK reasoning and the BCDR gap analysis that follows.

The Incident Captain's Role

The Incident Captain plays a central role in this framework. Rather than hosting a generic tabletop, the Captain designs each campaign with deliberate intent — anchoring the scenario to a known threat actor profile and the Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI) profile of the organization being simulated. This tests whether analysts are versed in the TTPs, tooling, and behavioral patterns of threat actors relevant to their organization, and surfaces gaps in detection maturity, response readiness, and threat intelligence awareness that would otherwise remain invisible until a real incident occurs. The +3 bonus procedures are pre-assigned by the Captain accordingly, rewarding analytical prioritization rooted in threat intelligence rather than guesswork. At the conclusion of the session, the Captain obtains structured feedback on where the team filled gaps effectively and where critical blind spots remain — making every session a deliberate gap analysis instrument.

The Worksheet as the Analytical Backbone

The Security Incident Analysis Worksheet serves as the structured output of the entire exercise. It is populated progressively through gameplay, with each game element feeding a specific section:

  • Section 2 — Incident Description is derived from the Game Conclusion. The Game Conclusion is the only point in the exercise where the complete attack story is revealed — making it the authoritative source for a fully attributed incident description.
  • Section 3 — Direct Impacts (BIA Lens) is informed by the Game Start scenario and the incident cards. Each stage is mapped to its corresponding critical function disruption, cascading downstream effects, and the RTOs and RPOs that were implicitly violated.
  • Section 5 — Incident Timeline is reconstructed from the chronological sequence of incident cards, cross-referenced against the Procedure Log. Gaps between occurrence and detection become detection lag annotations, each paired with a BCDR gap assessment.
  • Section 8 — Vulnerabilities and Techniques is populated from incident cards and successful procedure narratives. Each incident card encodes the specific TTP and maps directly to a MITRE ATT&CK technique ID.
  • Section 9 — Incident Resolution is evaluated through the sequence of procedure cards played and their IR phases. Critically, this section assesses whether containment and recovery actions were executed in a forensically sound manner — specifically whether chain of custody was maintained and whether the speed of recovery may have compromised the legal admissibility of digital evidence.
  • Section 12 — Lessons Learned is the synthesis of everything the game produced: procedure successes, procedure failures by category, undiscovered attack stages, time lost across IR phases, and the Legal and Ethical Challenges in Cyber Forensics dimension — addressing chain of custody, legal admissibility, privacy obligations, ethical tensions between containment speed and evidence preservation, law enforcement engagement, and regulatory disclosure.

The Integrated Disciplines

The framework integrates the following interconnected disciplines across the worksheet, each of which is explicitly demonstrated through gameplay rather than assumed:

  • Business Impact Analysis (BIA) — mapping critical functions disrupted at each stage of the attack chain to downstream operational, financial, and reputational consequences
  • Contingency Planning — assessed through procedure failures and undiscovered attack stages, revealing gaps in incident playbooks, rollback procedures, shutdown triggers, and activation criteria
  • BCDR — evaluated through the resolution of IR phases, alternate processing, failover capabilities, and whether RTOs and RPOs were implicitly met or violated. BCDR is a subset of contingency planning
  • ISCP Activation and Notification Phase — tested through the sequence and timeliness of procedure card selection and whether the team escalated appropriately within a timeframe a mature ISCP would prescribe
  • ISCP Recovery Phase and the 5 Rs — reflected in how systems were prioritized for restoration and whether regional readiness, response, resiliency, recovery, and reconstitution principles were applied
  • Human Controls and SETA — surfaced through Personnel-category procedure failures revealing analyst training gaps, fatigue, and missed threat intelligence connections
  • Legal and Ethical Challenges in Cyber Forensics — chain of custody, legal admissibility, privacy obligations, ethical tensions between rapid containment and evidence preservation, law enforcement engagement, and regulatory disclosure
  • Lessons Learned — synthesized from procedure successes, failures by category, undiscovered attack stages, time lost across IR phases, and legal and forensic gap analysis

The Procedure Log as the Bridge

The Procedure Log is the real-time recording instrument that sits at the center of this framework, bridging the tabletop exercise and the Security Incident Analysis Worksheet. Every procedure card played is logged here during gameplay. After the session, this log becomes the primary source material for populating Section 2 (Incident Description via Game Conclusion), Section 3 (Direct Impacts via Game Start and incident cards), Section 5 (Incident Timeline), Section 8 (Vulnerabilities and Techniques), Section 9 (Incident Resolution), and Section 12 (Lessons Learned) simultaneously. Undiscovered attack stages — rows in the log with no attack stage revealed — become the most significant lessons learned entries, representing structural gaps in monitoring, logging, or tooling that no analyst skill could have compensated for during play.

Action Cards and IR Phase Mapping

The action cards are not chosen at random — every procedure selection should reflect deliberate prioritization, not guesswork. Each card type maps directly to a real-world IR phase, and the sequence in which they are played is as analytically significant as which cards are played. This phase is about decision convergence under uncertainty:

On the +3 Bonus

The +3 bonus is pre-assigned by the Incident Captain before the game starts, based on the campaign design. It identifies the procedure cards most statistically likely to reveal part of the attack chain for that specific scenario. Players do not know which procedures carry the bonus until they play them — this is an intentional mechanic that rewards analytical prioritization rather than random selection. The +3 is not a standalone analytical category and requires no separate entry in Section 12. Its value to the worksheet is already captured under Procedure Successes and Failures — a +3 procedure that succeeded is documented under successes, one that failed is documented under failures with a root cause category.

Chronological IR Phase Ordering

When selecting procedure cards, the goal is to broadly respect the chronological order of IR phases — Detection and Analysis, Containment, Eradication, Recovery. The important principle is not rigid sequential execution but rather that no phase should be skipped entirely or bypassed prematurely. In practice, Detection and Analysis often run in parallel with early Containment actions — that is acceptable and realistic. What the game is specifically designed to surface is the cost of jumping to Containment or Eradication before sufficient investigation has occurred. Acting too early wastes time, may contaminate evidence, and can cause the threat actor to go quiet rather than be fully eradicated. The +3 bonus procedures should therefore be evaluated not only for their likelihood of revealing an incident card but for where they fall in the IR phase timeline — a high-value procedure played out of sequence still carries a cost.

This does not mean a single investigative procedure is limited to revealing only the earliest stage of the attack — a well-chosen procedure card played during the Detection phase can surface incident cards spanning multiple stages of the attack chain simultaneously.

Procedure Cards and Attack Chain Coverage

A single procedure card can reveal more than one incident card depending on the scope of its investigative reach. For example, in an insider threat scenario, endpoint analysis may reveal not only the insider threat event itself but also adjacent events such as a new privileged user being added — both of which are part of the threat actor's broader strategy and overall action on objectives. Procedure cards with this wider coverage across the attack chain are the ones most deserving of the +3 designation, and the Incident Captain assigns the bonus accordingly. This is why procedure card selection should never be treated as an isolated decision — each card played should be evaluated for what it might reveal across the full attack chain, not just the immediate next step.

Threat Intelligence as an Incident Captain Tool

The Incident Captain can deliberately design the campaign around a known threat actor profile — one the organization has previously encountered or that is relevant to their industry. By doing so, the Captain introduces a hidden test: how many players are versed in that threat actor's known TTPs, tooling, and behavioral patterns, and how well do they understand the threat intelligence profile of the organization they are defending? Players who draw on that threat intelligence knowledge will naturally prioritize the procedure cards most likely to uncover multiple linked stages of the attack chain in a single play — and those are precisely the procedures the Captain has pre-assigned the +3 bonus to. The correlation between threat actor knowledge and procedure card effectiveness becomes visible through gameplay, and the degree to which players recognize or miss that connection feeds directly into the lessons learned section as a measurable assessment of analyst readiness against known threat actor profiles.

Correlation to Time and Lessons Learned

The sequence of procedure cards played and whether they were played in a broadly optimal order can all be used to assess how efficiently the incident was handled. Time spent across IR phases becomes a measurable output feeding the lessons learned section across three specific dimensions: detection maturity, response readiness, and analyst familiarity with the threat actor profile.

Critically, time lost is not always a personnel or training failure. Each procedure failure should be categorized by its root cause — Technical, Financial, Political, or Personnel — because the category determines the remediation path.

Finally, any attack stage that no procedure uncovered during the game represents the most significant lessons learned finding of all — not af failed attempt, but a complete absence of detection capability. These undiscovered stages reveal structural gaps in monitoring, logging, or tooling that no amount of analyst skill could have compensated for, and they feed directly into the BCDR and contingency planning gap analysis in Section 12.

The Outcome

Analysts and students leave each session with a completed or substantially populated Security Incident Analysis Worksheet that connects simulated gameplay directly to real-world BCDR, contingency planning, BIA, cyber forensics, and threat-informed defense principles. The Incident Captain leaves with a structured picture of team capability, threat intelligence awareness, forensic readiness, and organizational gap areas that informs future training priorities. Each session is repeatable, scalable across different incident types and threat actor profiles, and directly aligned with the analytical standards expected in both academic and professional incident response contexts. What began as a card game becomes a structured, intelligence-driven gap analysis instrument — one that produces professional-grade analytical outputs from every tabletop session conducted.
